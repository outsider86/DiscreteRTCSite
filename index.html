DiscreteRTC: Discrete Diffusion Policies
are Natural Asynchronous Executors

Pengcheng Wang1, Kaiwen Hong2, Chensheng Peng1, Katherine Driggs-Campbell2, Masayoshi Tomizuka1, Chenfeng Xu3, Chen Tang4
1UC Berkeley, 2UIUC, 3UT Austin, 4UCLA
arXiv Code

Abstract

As physical AI models grow, their inference latency becomes a critical obstacle for real-time control. Asynchronous execution hides this latency by letting a policy think while acting, and real-time chunking (RTC) removes inter-chunk discontinuity by posing chunk transitions as inpainting: freezing committed actions and generating the rest consistently. However, RTC with a flow-matching head is structurally suboptimal: its inpainting relies on inference-time gradient corrections rather than the base policy, yielding no pre-training benefit, a fine-tuning burden, hand-tuned guidance weights, and extra per-step cost that inflates the very latency RTC aims to hide. We observe that discrete diffusion policies are natural asynchronous executors: inpainting is their native operation, resolving all four limitations, while early stopping provides adaptive guidance and reduces inference cost. We propose DiscreteRTC, which replaces gradient-based inpainting with native unmasking, and show on dynamic simulation and real-world manipulation tasks that it outperforms continuous RTC and other baselines in success rate while being simpler and faster.

Flow-Matching is Not Suitable for RTC

Interactive Visualization: Continuous RTC (Flow-Matching)

Despite its elegance, RTC with a flow-matching action head is far from ideal. We systematically analyze its limitations and show that they stem from the same root cause: the inpainting capability comes from the inference-time correction (e.g., ΠGDM), not from the base policy.

Key Limitations

(a) Pre-training without Inpainting. The flow policy is not pre-trained on action chunks with inconsistent noise levels, so scaling pre-training does not directly improve asynchronous performance.
(b) Fine-tuning Required. Adequate inpainting quality demands a dedicated fine-tuning stage with techniques such as action-suffix conditioning, adding training complexity and risking interference with the base generation quality.
(c) Heuristic Guidance Weights. The soft-masking schedule and gradient clipping threshold are hand-designed and must be re-tuned per task and model.
(d) Extra Inference Cost. Computing the guidance term at every denoising step roughly doubles computation, ironically increasing the very latency RTC aims to hide.

Discrete Diffusion Policies are Natural Asynchronous Executors

Interactive Visualization: Discrete RTC (Ours)

We show that discrete diffusion policies naturally resolve the limitations of flow-matching-based RTC. Since inpainting is the native operation of discrete diffusion — given a partially masked token sequence, the model predicts and unmasks the missing tokens — no architectural modification, no additional loss term, and no inference-time guidance are needed.

Key Advantages

(a) Pre-trained with Inpainting. Scaling pre-training directly benefits asynchronous execution, since inpainting is structurally identical to standard masked generation — larger models and more data yield better generation.
(b) Fine-tuning Free. Since the inpainting capability is acquired during pre-training, a discrete diffusion policy can be deployed for asynchronous execution immediately, with no inpainting-specific fine-tuning.
(c) Natural Guidance Weights. The early-stopping mechanism provides natural guidance via the number of unmasked tokens — the remaining partially unmasked actions serve as an adaptive guidance signal for the next inference, replacing heuristic soft-masking schedules.
(d) Lower Inference Cost. Since unmasking starts from an intermediate state and ends at an intermediate stage, the total number of unmasking steps is reduced to roughly s/H of the original. Instead of increasing inference cost as in continuous RTC (2.5x), DiscreteRTC actually reduces it (~0.4x).

Experiments

Simulated Benchmark: Kinetix

Across all inference delays, DiscreteRTC consistently outperforms ContinuousRTC and other variants on both solve rates and throughputs, showing the great advantages of the native inpainting capability in discrete diffusion policies. Moreover, as shown in Figure 4, DiscreteRTC requires fewer iterative steps than ContinuousRTC, especially under large delays with execution horizon as discussed in Sec. 4.2 of the paper.

Figure 5 presents the experimental results of more variants in Kinetix:

  • vs. Training-time Continuous RTC: Under varying delays, DiscreteRTC outperforms Training-time Continuous RTC, which is fine-tuned with an explicit objective and recipe to improve inpainting, further demonstrating the strong performance of the fine-tuning free DiscreteRTC.
  • DiscreteRTC + Fixed Steps: By using fixed unmasking steps during inference, this variant achieves better performance by generating more fine-grained actions with the same computation budget.
  • DiscreteVLASH: Integrating with VLASH achieves more stable performance across varying delays but at the cost of performance drop with smaller delays. This demonstrates that DiscreteRTC can be seamlessly combined with advanced inference-time methods for further gains.

Real-World Results

We design two dynamical tasks to stress reactiveness: Dynamic Pick and Dynamic Place, where the robot must pick a moving object or place it onto a moving platform. This requires the robot to continuously re-estimate the object pose and execute smoothly.

In terms of inference time, the two asynchronous methods move in opposite directions: ContinuousRTC inflates flow-matching cost from 151 to 256 ms, a near-1.7× overhead from ΠGDM, whereas DiscreteRTC reduces discrete diffusion cost from 303 to 206 ms with fewer tokens to unmask. Discussion

Conclusions. We presented DiscreteRTC, which exploits the native inpainting capability of discrete diffusion for asynchronous real-time control, eliminating the need for inpainting-specific fine-tuning, heuristic guidance weights, and extra inference cost inherent to flow-matching-based RTC. Simulated and real-world experiments show that DiscreteRTC outperforms ContinuousRTC and other asynchronous baselines, and can be combined with methods such as VLASH for further gains.

Limitations. We note several limitations of our current instantiation, each coinciding with an active research direction whose progress will directly benefit DiscreteRTC:

  1. Our naive k-bin quantization ignores temporal structure and yields overly long token sequences, motivating compact, temporally aware tokenizers.
  2. Our modularized AR-VLM + discrete diffusion head prevents the backbone from participating in iterative unmasking, a gap that unified discrete-diffusion VLAs aim to close.
  3. Traditional max-confidence unmasking does not yet translate the natural schedule into consistent gains.

Future Steps. These limitations point to three concrete directions:

  1. A time-causally ordered action tokenizer that produces temporally ordered, compact token representations.
  2. A unified discrete diffusion VLA in which observation reasoning and action generation share the same backbone.
  3. An appropriate yet principled unmasking strategy that fully unleashes the potential of the natural schedule — emerging techniques such as AR-block decoding align better with the implicit autoregressive structure induced by RTC and integrate seamlessly.
